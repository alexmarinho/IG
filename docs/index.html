Story · why order matters

The order of the jobs determines the total cost.

An optimal order exists, but there are too many orders to enumerate. Iterated Greedy builds one order and improves it by iteration.

Order and cost

The same five jobs in two orders.

The cost of an order is the sum of the setups between families and the weighted tardiness. Waiting for a release date delays every later job.

Enumeration

The time needed to enumerate every order.

Enumerating a 28-job instance at 10⁹ orders per second takes longer than the age of the universe.

The method

The destroy–rebuild loop.

An Iterated Greedy engine running in this page on a seeded 12-job, 4-family instance (mulberry32, seed 0xce29767d). Greedy, because every move takes the cheapest insertion; iterated, because the destroy–rebuild cycle repeats. Every insertion position is re-evaluated against the complete objective. The five steps: construct by best insertion, destroy three jobs, measure every insertion on the trial track, reject at break-even when no position beats the rejection cost, accept or revert.

    Future · where it goes

    A tested baseline — and what comes next.

    This is a reproducible baseline against the 2015 study; the next steps extend the search and the model, one validated change at a time.

    Current evidence

    Exact evaluatorImplementedCanonical

    Fixed-point objective pricing, Rust/Python golden agreement and a built browser WASM.

    Adaptive destructionMeasuredExperimental

    Reached the recorded best values in two stagnation cases; aggressive ramps hurt fine-grained cases.

    GPU replica fleetMeasuredExperimental

    Exact pricing verified on an RTX 3090: 16× the replicas cost 22% more wall clock at n=30, but 4.2× at n=200. One optimized Rust core still wins single-instance speed.

    Learned removal operatorsMeasuredExperimental

    Evolved operators survive unseen seeds but not unseen instances: +0.6 pp against random removal on the training set, −1.3 pp held out, across three models.

    The next problem

    The static benchmark is effectively closed — exact methods now prove optimality on it, so a metaheuristic can only tie. The open problem sits one step earlier and closer to a real shop floor: orders arrive over time, each processing time and due date is only an estimate, and every accept-or-reject is committed on the spot, before the future is known.

    Online order acceptance
    Reoptimizing policy · profit
    0
    replans the whole schedule at every arrival
    Myopic greedy · profit
    0
    accepts every order that still fits
    job families sequence-dependent setup now rejected order (lost forever)

    The greedy policy takes every order it can fit and later has no room for the profitable ones. The reoptimizing policy sometimes rejects a cheap order to protect capacity, then accepts a valuable one that arrives afterward. Each decision is final and is taken without knowledge of future arrivals, which requires an exact evaluator fast enough to reprice the schedule inside the decision window.

    One incumbentOpen

    Online acceptance with rejection has essentially one direct paper — theory-first, with no fast reoptimizing engine and no setups.

    Make-to-order realityApplied

    Quoting accept-or-reject the moment an order lands, under uncertain times, is what make-to-order shops actually do — and efficiency here is measurable day to day.

    Reoptimize per arrivalFit

    At millions of exact evaluations per second, replanning everything before answering is no longer too slow.

    A thin literature is ambiguous: it can mean “open and wanted” or “empty because nobody cares.” The evidence here is that the slot is empty, not that the community is asking for it — that ambiguity is the real risk of this direction, and it is named on purpose.

    Question
    When does exact local repair repay its solver overhead?
    Change
    Pass a bounded removed-job neighbourhood to an exact repair model.
    Comparator
    Current lowest-cost reinsertion under the same outer search budget.
    Success criterion
    Higher improvement per millisecond and lower time-to-target.
    Evidence
    Repair-size sweeps, solver overhead and exactness checks on small neighbourhoods.

    Which gains survive when jobs also choose a machine?

    Open multi-machine OAS data and exact small solutions.
    Success criterion
    Feasible schedules, exact agreement on small cases and competitive public-benchmark quality.

    Open directions, runnable

    The two experimental rows above ship as a companion notebook instead of as assertions. It detects the runtime it lands on, re-verifies exact pricing against the CPU engine before reporting any timing, and recomputes every number on each run — including the ones that do not favour it.

    A · Breadth

    GPU replica fleet

    Thousands of independent searches advance in lockstep as one tensor program. While the step is bound by kernel-launch overhead, width is almost free — and stops being free as the instance grows, which is the honest boundary of the idea. Runs on CPU with identical numbers when no GPU is present.

    Open in ColabRead the fleet
    B · Discovery

    Learned removal operators

    An evolutionary loop proposes removal operators and re-scores the winner on fresh seeds and held-out instances. The offline backend needs no API key, and it reproduces the recorded outcome: a healthy training gain that turns into a loss against uniform random removal once the split is honest.
